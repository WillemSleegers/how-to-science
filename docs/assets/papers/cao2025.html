Cao et al. (2025) fine-tune LLMs to simulate country-level survey response distributions across the World Values Survey, framing the task as predicting a full probability distribution over response options rather than a single answer. Fine-tuning with KL divergence loss substantially outperforms zero-shot prompting across all seven models tested, and the approach generalises to unseen countries and to an entirely different survey (Pew Global Attitudes). A key limitation is that all LLMs — fine-tuned or not — produce less diverse predictions across countries than real human data, suggesting homogenisation is a structural problem rather than a prompting failure.

Claim — The paper fine-tunes LLMs using first-token probability alignment with KL divergence loss to simulate country-level survey response distributions — distinct from prompting approaches that elicit single answers. Quote — “we devise a fine-tuning method based on first-token probabilities to minimize divergence between predicted and actual response distributions for a given question” Page — 3141

Claim — Fine-tuning substantially outperforms zero-shot prompting across all seven tested models, for both seen and unseen countries and questions (e.g., 34.3% 1-JSD increase for Llama3-8B-Instruct). Quote — “Across seven LLMs from three model families, our fine-tuning method substantially boosts prediction accuracy on seen and unseen WVS countries and questions.” Page — 3141

Claim — The best fine-tuned models achieve average 1-JSD scores around 0.81–0.82, compared to 0.61–0.75 for zero-shot baselines. Quote — (Table 4, e.g., Llama3-8B-Instruct FT Avg. = 0.823; ZS Avg. = 0.613) Page — 3145

Claim — Unseen questions are harder to generalise to than unseen countries; models transfer to new countries more readily than to new question types. Quote — “unseen questions (Q3) tend to present a greater challenge than unseen countries (C2 or C3), as indicated by slightly worse scores for unseen questions (e.g., 0.781 vs. 0.886 1−JSD for Llama3-Instruct).” Page — 3146

Claim — Fine-tuned models generalise to an entirely unseen survey (Pew Global Attitudes Survey), achieving 19.1% and 27.4% accuracy improvements over zero-shot on two dataset splits. Quote — “Notably, all metric scores show significant improvements for both models after fine-tuning… Llama3 (FT) achieves 19.1% and 27.4% improvement for two datasets, respectively.” Page — 3148

Claim — All LLMs tested — fine-tuned or not — produce less diverse predictions across countries than actual human survey data, raising concerns about utility for cross-national research. Quote — “we find that all LLMs we tested, whether fine-tuned or not, are less diverse in their predictions across countries than the actual human survey data.” Page — 3141–3142

Claim — Base models show high uniformity across countries before fine-tuning, indicating limited sensitivity to national cultural variation; fine-tuning improves but does not fully resolve this. Quote — “the outputs of Base models exhibit a high degree of uniformity across countries, indicating a limited sensitivity to national variations when addressing cultural values questions. After fine-tuning, there is a slight reduction in 1−JSD, suggesting an enhancement in the responsiveness to diverse cultural contexts.” Page — 3147

Claim — The framing is explicit: the goal is to simulate human population responses as a tool for cross-national social science research where full fieldwork is infeasible, not to probe LLM values. Quote — “LLM simulations of survey responses, if accurate towards the corresponding populations, could accelerate social science research and aid in more informed policy decisions.” Page — 3141

Claim — The study uses only English prompts and country names to represent cultures, limiting applicability to non-English LLMs and fine-grained within-country cultural contexts. Quote — “Our study only uses English prompts for experiments and uses countries to represent specific cultures… While this approach offers some valuable insights, it may limit the applicability of our findings to non-English LLMs and diverse fine-grained cultural contexts.” Page — 3149

Claim — Even the best fine-tuned models struggle substantially on unseen questions; systematic inaccuracies remain. Quote — “While even our best models struggle with the task, especially on unseen questions, our results demonstrate the benefits of specialization for simulation” Page — 3141
