Lind et al. (2001)

Full citation: Lind, L. H., Schober, M. F., & Conrad, F. G. (2001). Clarifying question meaning in a web-based survey. Proceedings of the Annual Meeting of the American Statistical Association, August 5–9, 2001.

Study overview

  • N: 48 paid respondents
  • Sample: Recruited from New York City area and New School University community via Village Voice ad; 27 women, 21 men; mean age 29.8 years; ethnicities, educational backgrounds, and computer experience balanced across groups. Lab study, not a field survey.
  • Mode: Web-based (lab setting)
  • Survey topic: Two government surveys — Current Point of Purchase Survey (purchases) and CPI-Housing (housing); 10 questions total (5 each)
  • Clarification type: Hyperlinked full definitions available via click for all conditions. Key manipulation was whether a partial definition was embedded in the question wording itself.
  • Scenario method: Respondents answered based on fictional paper-based scenarios (floor plans, receipts, short stories), allowing precise measurement of response accuracy and control over whether a definition component was relevant to the respondent’s situation.

Design

3 between-subjects conditions:

  1. Original wording — question as normally worded, no partial definition embedded; full definition available via hyperlink
  2. Relevant rewording — question reworded to include part of the definition directly relevant to the respondent’s scenario (resolves their specific ambiguity); full definition still available via hyperlink
  3. Irrelevant rewording — question reworded to include part of the definition not relevant to the respondent’s scenario; full definition still available via hyperlink

Scenarios were either straightforward (easy to answer correctly without definitions) or complicated (atypical mapping onto survey definitions, hard to answer correctly without clarification).

Key findings

Definition request rates (complicated scenarios only)

  • Original wording: 25.0% of questions
  • Relevant rewording: 21.4% (not significantly different from original — ambiguity already resolved by embedded text)
  • Irrelevant rewording: 42.7% — about twice as often, as if the irrelevant information signaled to respondents that they needed clarification

Response accuracy

Condition Straightforward Complicated
Original wording 93.7% 42.2%
Relevant rewording 93.7% 84.9%
Irrelevant rewording 93.7% 47.4%
  • Relevant information in the question dramatically improved accuracy for complicated scenarios
  • Irrelevant information did not improve accuracy despite higher definition-seeking rates — respondents may have been discouraged by the irrelevant content and not read definitions thoroughly

Response time per question

  • Original wording: 23.4 seconds
  • Relevant rewording: 32.3 seconds
  • Irrelevant rewording: 33.5 seconds
  • Reworded questions increased survey duration regardless of whether the embedded information was useful

Key conclusions

  • Embedding relevant definitional content directly in questions improves accuracy when it addresses the respondent’s actual ambiguity
  • Embedding irrelevant definitional content increases survey duration and definition-seeking without improving accuracy
  • Clarification only helps when it is matched to the respondent’s situation

Relevance to tooltip usage

Demonstrates that whether a tooltip/definition improves data quality depends on whether its content is relevant to the specific respondent. Irrelevant definitions increase burden without benefit. This motivates careful targeting of definitions rather than adding them broadly.
