Tooltip Usage

Warning

This chapter is still a work in progress.

Surveys often include tooltips — small pop-up explanations attached to terms, response options, or questions — to provide additional context without cluttering the survey interface. A key question is whether respondents actually read and use these tooltips, and whether they improve response quality.

What Are Tooltips?

Tooltips in online surveys typically appear as clickable or hoverable elements (e.g., an info icon or underlined term) that reveal a brief explanation when activated. They are commonly used to define technical or ambiguous terms, clarify the intent of a question, or provide examples and anchors for response options.

Note that the survey methodology literature rarely uses the term “tooltip.” The dominant terminology is clarification features, definitions, rollover definitions, or help text. These are functionally the same thing.

The underlying assumption is that tooltips reduce respondent confusion without burdening attentive respondents who do not need the extra information. Whether this holds depends on whether respondents actually use tooltips when they need them.

Do Respondents Use Tooltips?

Usage rates are consistently low when accessing a tooltip requires a deliberate action. Conrad et al. (2006) embedded definitions in a web survey and found that respondents requested definitions on only 13.8% of occasions when they could have done so. The access method matters greatly: a rollover/hover interaction produced a 36.5% usage rate, compared to 8.9% for a single click and 6.5% for a two-click interaction. Each additional step required halved usage.

Peytchev et al. (2010) confirmed and extended these findings in a larger experiment (N = 2,481). They compared three conditions: no definitions, rollover definitions, and always-on definitions displayed below each question. Usage rates were:

  • Always-on: 60.7% of respondents consulted definitions (self-report)
  • Rollover: 45.4% (behavioral log data), 35.6% (self-report)

An important and counterintuitive finding from Peytchev et al. (2010) was that respondents explicitly trained to use definitions actually used them less (38%) compared to untrained respondents (53%). The authors suggest that deliberate instruction makes respondents more aware of the effort involved, leading them to opt out.

Lind et al. (2001) found similar variability: in a lab setting, usage ranged from ~23% with minimal instructions to ~81% with stronger framing — demonstrating how sensitive usage is to the surrounding context.

A key problem underlying all of these findings is that respondents often do not recognize that their interpretation of a question differs from the intended meaning (Conrad & Schober, 2000). They therefore do not seek clarification even when it is available, because they do not know they need it.

Does Tooltip Usage Affect Response Quality?

When respondents do consult definitions, the evidence suggests it improves accuracy. Conrad & Schober (2000) showed in a telephone survey context that conversational clarification led to responses more consistent with official definitions. In web surveys, Peytchev et al. (2010) found that always-on definitions changed response patterns on 4 of 8 questions tested (compared to control), while rollover definitions changed 2 of 8. Respondents in the definitions conditions also spent more time on questions — ~60 seconds more for always-on, ~7 seconds more for rollover — suggesting genuine reading rather than skipping.

Lind et al. (2001) found that clarification only improved accuracy when the definition was specifically relevant to the respondent’s situation. Including definitional information that did not apply to a respondent increased survey duration without improving data quality.

The key qualification is that response quality improvements depend entirely on whether respondents actually access the definitions. Under typical click-to-reveal designs, most respondents do not.

Recommendations

  • Avoid click-to-reveal tooltips for important clarifications. Usage rates under click-based access are around 10–15%. If a clarification is important for response accuracy, relying on respondents to seek it out is unlikely to work.

  • Prefer always-on display for critical definitions. Displaying definitions below or beside the question item, rather than hiding them behind an interaction, approximately doubles usage and has a measurable effect on response patterns.

  • Hover/rollover is a middle ground. It achieves roughly 3–4x the usage of click-based access while keeping the interface less cluttered than always-on display. However, hover is unavailable on mobile devices, which increasingly account for a large share of survey respondents.

  • Do not expect training to compensate for poor tooltip design. Explicit instructions to use definitions do not reliably increase usage and can reduce it.
