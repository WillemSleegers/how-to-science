When presenting a Likert-type scale, you must choose which end of the scale to present first. On a horizontal scale, should “Strongly agree” appear on the left or “Strongly disagree”? On a vertical scale, which appears at the top?

## Mechanisms

Two mechanisms have been proposed to explain why response order can affect answers.

**Satisficing** <span data-cite-id="krosnick1991" data-cite-quote="When response alternatives are presented to respondents visually, either on a showcard or in a self-administered questionnaire, weak satisficing is likely to bias respondents toward selecting response alternatives presented early in a list." data-cite-page="216" class="cite-ref">(Krosnick, 1991)</span> — when questions are difficult or motivation is low, respondents may select the first acceptable option without fully evaluating the rest. In visual surveys this biases responses toward earlier options; in telephone surveys toward later ones, since the last option heard remains most accessible in memory.

**Anchoring-and-adjustment** <span data-cite-id="yan2015" data-cite-quote="Thus, we conclude that in our study, the observed scale-direction effects are largely attributable to respondents' use of the anchoring-and-adjustment heuristic." data-cite-page="162" class="cite-ref">(Yan & Keusch, 2015)</span> — respondents anchor on the first option and adjust insufficiently as they work through the remaining alternatives, biasing responses toward the starting point of the scale.

## Summary

In self-administered surveys—whether CAWI or PAPI—a primacy effect is reliably observed: the first response option in a sequence attracts disproportionate endorsement. For agree–disagree scales, placing “Strongly agree” first inflates agreement relative to placing “Strongly disagree” first. In telephone (CATI) surveys, recency effects are more common. The effect is amplified for speeders and less motivated respondents <span class="cite-ref" data-cite-id="keusch2018" data-cite-quote="" data-cite-page="">(Keusch, 2018; Malhotra, 2008)</span>, older respondents (65+) <span class="cite-ref" data-cite-id="knauper1999" data-cite-quote="" data-cite-page="">(Knauper, 1999)</span>, non-attitudinal items, and longer scales <span class="cite-ref" data-cite-id="yan2018" data-cite-quote="" data-cite-page="">(Yan et al., 2018)</span>. Switching to **item-specific response options** substantially reduces susceptibility to scale direction effects <span class="cite-ref" data-cite-id="hohne2018" data-cite-quote="" data-cite-page="">(J. K. Höhne & Krebs, 2018)</span> and also improves reliability and validity <span class="cite-ref" data-cite-id="saris2010a" data-cite-quote="" data-cite-page="">(Dykema et al., 2022; Saris et al., 2010)</span>. When agree–disagree scales are unavoidable, a negative-to-positive ordering (Disagree→Agree) is recommended for self-administered surveys to reduce the risk of inflated agreement <span class="cite-ref" data-cite-id="yonniechyung2018" data-cite-quote="" data-cite-page="">(Wöhner & Franzen, 2024; Yonnie Chyung et al., 2018)</span>.
