name: peng2025
description: Pre-registered mega-study of digital twins across 19 studies and 164 outcomes; finds average twin-human correlation ~0.2, systematic under-dispersion, and population mean bias — twins not ready for systematic deployment
type: reference
---

Peng et al. (2025) run a pre-registered mega-study of 19 experiments testing whether LLM-powered digital twins — built from rich individual-level data — can simulate real people's survey responses. Across 164 outcomes, the average twin-human correlation is only 0.197, twin responses are significantly less variable than human responses in 94% of outcomes, and population mean estimates are biased in 76% of outcomes. Adding more personal information about an individual improves the twin's ability to distinguish between people but does not improve individual-level accuracy or population mean estimation. The authors conclude that digital twins are "not ready for prime time" and should be thought of as hyper-rational versions of humans rather than clones.

**Claim** — Individual-level accuracy averages 75%, but this is misleading: a random benchmark achieves 63%, so the margin above chance is narrow.
**Quote** — "Across all outcomes, the average individual-level accuracy is 0.748, which compares favorably to the human test-retest accuracy of 0.817 reported by [16]... However, we note that a random benchmark (selecting answers from a uniform distribution on the relevant range) achieves an individual accuracy level of 0.629, suggesting that the absolute levels of individual-level accuracy should be interpreted with caution."
**Page** — 6–7

**Claim** — The correlation between twin and human responses averages only 0.197; positive in 96% of outcomes but rarely strong.
**Quote** — "The correlation between twin and human responses is positive in 157 of the 164 outcomes (95.7%), and it is significantly positive with p<0.05 in 128 of these cases (78.0%). The overall average correlation is 0.197."
**Page** — 7

**Claim** — Full digital twins using rich individual data do not achieve meaningfully higher individual-level accuracy than simple demographic-only personas.
**Quote** — "twins built from full personas achieve only slightly higher individual-level accuracy than those based only on demographic information (p=0.37)."
**Page** — 8

**Claim** — More personal information improves correlation (heterogeneity capture) but not individual-level accuracy or population mean estimation.
**Quote** — "enriching digital twins with detailed individual-level information improves our ability to capture heterogeneity across participants and predict relative differences between them... However, it does not substantially improve our ability to reproduce the exact responses given by specific participants, nor does it enhance predictions of population-level averages."
**Page** — 8

**Claim** — The distinction between improved correlation and unchanged individual accuracy is dramatic: in one example, correlation rises from 0.105 to 0.555 with full personas while accuracy is virtually unchanged (0.892 vs. 0.907).
**Quote** — "These plots clearly illustrate how the correlation is much improved when full personas are used (r_demo = 0.105 vs. r_full = .555). Yet, individual-level accuracy is virtually unchanged (accuracy_demo = 0.892 vs. accuracy_full = 0.907)."
**Page** — 8–9

**Claim** — Twin responses are systematically biased: average responses differ from humans by 0.35 standard deviations, with a statistically significant difference in 76% of outcomes.
**Quote** — "average twin responses differ from human responses by 0.352 standard deviations on average; a paired t-test finds a statistically significant difference (p<0.05) between humans and digital twins in 125 out of 164 outcomes (76.2%)."
**Page** — 7

**Claim** — Twin responses are systematically less variable than human responses: lower standard deviations in 93.9% of outcomes, statistically significant in 89% of those cases.
**Quote** — "the standard deviation of the twin responses is lower than that of human responses in 154 of 164 cases (93.9%), indicating under-dispersion in twin responses... This difference is statistically significant (p<0.05) in 146 of those 154 cases."
**Page** — 7

**Claim** — Digital twins are more accurate for participants with higher education and income, and for moderate (non-extreme) political views — suggesting a WEIRD bias.
**Quote** — "Twins tend to be more accurate for participants with higher education levels and higher income, indicating a potential bias in favor of more socioeconomically advantaged individuals which could exacerbate a focus on WEIRD... populations in social sciences. Accuracy also tends to be higher for participants with moderate political views and religious attendance habits."
**Page** — 16

**Claim** — Twins perform worst in the political domain and are less capable in socially sensitive contexts, giving more socially desirable answers than humans.
**Quote** — "correlations were particularly low for outcomes in the political domain... the correlation was significantly lower for outcomes where social desirability was salient, suggesting twins are less capable of mimicking human responses in socially-sensitive contexts (a qualitative review suggests twins are more likely to provide socially-desirable responses)."
**Page** — 12–13

**Claim** — Twins exhibit systematic "pro-human" and "pro-technology" biases, and in cognitive domains display near-perfect knowledge and more rational behaviour than humans (hyper-accuracy distortion).
**Quote** — "twins also tended to express views that could be described as 'pro-technology'... In cognitive domains, consistent with the hyper-accuracy distortion identified by [19], twins tended to display perfect knowledge on questions with objectively correct answers, and they tended to behave more rationally than their human counterparts."
**Page** — 15

**Claim** — The data suggest a ceiling on achievable correlation regardless of method: above ~0.3 may be unrealistic given the nature of human opinion data.
**Quote** — "This also suggests that given the type of data we collected, it may be unrealistic to expect correlations higher than 0.3."
**Page** — 10

**Claim** — Digital twins should be understood as hyper-rational quasi-omniscient versions of humans, not clones, with values partly shaped by the base LLM.
**Quote** — "based on our results it may not be realistic to think about them as 'clones' of humans, but rather as hyper-rational, quasi-omniscient versions of humans, with implicit values partly imbued by their base LLM."
**Page** — 19

**Claim** — Digital twins are not ready for systematic deployment; significant improvements or calibration of expectations would be needed.
**Quote** — "we find that while digital twins show promise, they are not fully 'ready for prime time' yet. Significant improvements would be needed for twins to be ready for systematic deployment and/or expectations of digital twin performance may need to be appropriately calibrated."
**Page** — 17
