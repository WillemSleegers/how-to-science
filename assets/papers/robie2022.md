Robie, Meade, Risavy, and Rasheed (2022) examined whether varying the order of response options on a Likert-type personality scale would affect measurement. One thousand one hundred and ninety-eight Canadian university students completed the HEXACO personality inventory online via Qualtrics. Participants were randomly assigned to one of four response option order conditions: (1) standard incremental order (strongly disagree … strongly agree), (2) standard decremental order (strongly agree … strongly disagree), (3) randomly varying order where each item was independently assigned either incremental or decremental, and (4) totally randomized order where each response option was in a random position for each item.

Results showed little to no meaningful response option order effects on personality scale scores across the first three conditions. The scales demonstrated adequate reliability and structural validity regardless of whether the order was incremental, decremental, or randomly varying. The totally randomized condition (condition 4), however, produced evidence of increased careless responding — respondents in this condition were less consistent and showed lower data quality. The authors conclude that while response option order does not substantially bias personality measurement, completely randomizing the positions of individual response options increases responder burden and careless responding.

**Study details**
- Topic/construct: HEXACO personality traits (6 factors)
- Response options: 5-point horizontal Likert-type scale; 4 order conditions
- Scale format: Horizontal, web (Qualtrics)
- Design: Between-subjects randomization to 4 order conditions
- Sample: N = 1,198 Canadian university students
- Mode: Online (Qualtrics)
- Main finding: No meaningful order effects for incremental, decremental, or randomly varying per-item orderings; totally randomized order increases careless responding
