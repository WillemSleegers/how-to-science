# Conrad & Schober (2000)

**Full citation:** Conrad, F. G., & Schober, M. F. (2000). Clarifying question meaning in a household telephone survey. *Public Opinion Quarterly, 64*(1), 1–28.

## Study overview

- **N:** 227 respondents
- **Sample:** Nationally representative US household sample; telephone survey
- **Mode:** Telephone interview (not a web survey)
- **Survey topic:** Two surveys — CPI-Housing (questions about housing costs/rooms) and CPOPS (questions about consumer purchases)
- **Clarification type:** Conversational interviewing — interviewers proactively clarified question meaning or responded to respondent-initiated requests, providing official definitions (e.g., what counts as a "room," what counts as a "purchase")

## Design

Compared two conditions:
1. **Standardized interviewing** — interviewers gave neutral, non-directive responses to questions (e.g., "whatever it means to you")
2. **Conversational interviewing** — interviewers clarified the intended meaning of terms when relevant, using official definitions

## Key findings

- **Response change:** 21.8% of answers changed in the conversational condition vs. 11.0% in the standardized condition — roughly double the rate of response revision
- **Accuracy example (CPOPS):** 95% of purchases in the conversational condition were classified correctly as "legal purchases" (per official definition) vs. 57% in the standardized condition
- Respondents often do not realize their interpretation differs from the intended meaning, so they do not seek clarification even when it would help

## Relevance to tooltip usage

This is a telephone study, not a web/tooltip study. Its relevance is theoretical: it establishes that (1) respondents frequently misinterpret survey questions, and (2) when clarification is provided, response accuracy improves substantially. It motivates the rationale for making definitions available in web surveys.
