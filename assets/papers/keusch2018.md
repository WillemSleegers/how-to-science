Keusch and Yan (2018) examined scale direction effects on brand attitude ratings using data from an Austrian non-probability online panel (N = 502). Respondents rated attributes of a brand using 6-point end-labeled unipolar rating scales displayed in horizontal grid format. Scale direction was manipulated between subjects: half received a scale running from the positive (high) end to the negative (low) end (decremental), and half received the reverse (incremental).

A strong scale direction effect was found: respondents gave systematically higher ratings when the positive end appeared first (decremental direction). Crucially, the effect was present across all respondents but was stronger among those who completed the survey more quickly (speeders). This suggests that the effect is partly driven by satisficing — respondents who spend less time processing the scale are more susceptible to positional anchoring — but is not limited to speeders. Even careful respondents were somewhat influenced by scale direction. The finding that scale direction matters even for non-speeders is consistent with an anchoring-and-adjustment mechanism operating alongside satisficing.

**Claim** — Scale direction significantly affects brand attribute ratings, with higher ratings when the positive pole appears first; speeders show the largest effects but all respondents are affected.

**Study details**
- Topic/construct: Brand attribute ratings (multiple dimensions)
- Response options: 6-point end-labeled unipolar scale (high-to-low vs low-to-high)
- Scale format: Horizontal grid
- Design: Split-ballot between-subjects (decremental vs incremental scale direction)
- Sample: N = 502 Austrian non-probability online panel
- Mode: Web
- Main finding: Strong primacy effect; speeders show larger effects but effect present for all respondents; supports both satisficing and anchoring-and-adjustment
