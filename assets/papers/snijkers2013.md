# Snijkers et al. (2013)

**Full citation:** Snijkers, G., Haraldsen, G., Jones, J., & Willimack, D. K. (2013). *Designing and conducting business surveys*. Wiley.

## Overview

A comprehensive methodological book on business surveys by authors from Statistics Netherlands, Statistics Norway, the UK Office for National Statistics, and the US Census Bureau. Not an empirical paper — a practitioner-focused synthesis. Contains empirical examples throughout.

## Relevant sections

### Chapter 2: The Business Context and Its Implications for the Survey Response Process
- Business surveys use technical definitions that differ from everyday business language; respondents assume familiar terms mean what they ordinarily mean, even when the survey uses them differently
- Respondent selection is controlled by the business, not the survey organization — the person who answers may lack the expertise to interpret technical questions
- Business response is often a distributed, multi-person process: a "response coordinator" delegates data retrieval to others who may never see the questionnaire

### Section 8.4.2: Clarifying Terms and Tasks
Three sources of comprehension failure specific to business surveys:
1. **Ambiguity** — same terms used differently in survey vs. business contexts (e.g., "employment" means different things on a labor force survey vs. a business survey); respondents don't recognize the distinction
2. **Vagueness** — unclear spatiotemporal boundaries (e.g., reference periods, what to include/exclude); when definitions seem intuitive, respondents consider them unnecessary; when not intuitive, respondents tend not to read them — the same double-bind as in household surveys (citing Gerbner et al. 1996)
3. **Mapping discrepancies** — even when definitions are clear, respondents struggle to classify borderline cases (e.g., temporary workers, part-time staff)

Key quote: *"When definitions seem intuitive, respondents often consider them unnecessary, and when they are not intuitive, respondents tend not to read or follow them."*

### Section 8.4.3.4: Talking with Web Questionnaires
- Help icons (click-to-reveal) in business web surveys are described as "obtainable clarification" — but notes that help icons placed outside the respondent's foveal view are unlikely to be noticed
- References Conrad et al. (2007) for web-based dialogue clarification and Conrad & Schober (2010) for the broader case that dialogue improves data quality
- Proposes more sophisticated approaches: tailored help text triggered by paradata (response time, error frequency, answer corrections), or direct contact with a help desk

### Empirical example (Section 8.4.2)
Norwegian Structural Survey of Wholesale and Retail Trade: a question asking respondents to divide turnover across commercial categories (retail, wholesale, commission trade, etc.) had critical definitional information (e.g., betting commissions classified as services) buried in a separate instruction manual, not in the questionnaire. The authors demonstrate how category ordering and inline definitions could prevent misclassification.

## Relevance to tooltip usage

The main business-survey reference for this topic. Establishes that the comprehension problems motivating tooltip use in household surveys are present — and arguably worse — in business surveys, due to the mismatch between survey definitions and everyday business terminology, the distributed response process, and the heterogeneity of the business population. No empirical data on business survey tooltip usage rates specifically.
