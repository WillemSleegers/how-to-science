Knäuper (1999) reanalyzes 14 response-order experiments originally conducted by Schuman and Presser (1971–1980) to disentangle the separate contributions of respondent age and education to response order susceptibility. Prior work (Narayan and Krosnick 1996) had attributed larger response order effects among low-education respondents to reduced "cognitive sophistication." Because age and education are negatively correlated (r = −.23 to −.32 in these data), Knäuper argues the apparent education effect may in fact be an age effect.

**Study design and data**

The 14 experiments were all conducted as split-ballot telephone interviews on nationally representative U.S. samples (Survey Research Center, University of Michigan, 1971–1980). In every experiment, respondents received identical questions but the order of two or three response alternatives was reversed across two random half-samples (Form A vs. Form B). Questions covered attitudes on divorce, oil supply, housing adequacy, Vietnam aid, labor unions, open housing, and individual vs. social conditioning. Sample sizes ranged from N = 202 to N = 915 per experiment; all respondents were age 18 or older.

**Topic/construct measured:** Attitudes on a range of social/political topics (divorce, energy, housing, labor, foreign policy) — the construct of interest is not the attitude itself but the magnitude of response order effects as a function of respondent age and education.

**Response options:** Two or three labeled alternatives per question (e.g., "easier to obtain / more difficult to obtain / stay as it is now"; "plenty of oil / not enough oil"; "government should provide housing / each company on its own"). All options were fully labeled and read aloud by the interviewer.

**Scale format:** Auditory, read aloud over the telephone. No visual scale; response order manipulated by reversing the sequence in which alternatives were read.

**Mode:** Telephone interview (all 14 experiments).

**Sample:** Nationally representative U.S. adults (age 18+), multiple independent surveys 1971–1980.

**Main findings**

In 12 of 14 experiments, the absolute percentage difference by response order was larger for respondents aged 65+ than for respondents aged 18–64. Across all 14 experiments, the mean absolute response order effect was roughly twice as large for older respondents (13.87%) as for younger respondents (6.56%). A meta-analysis confirmed this age difference is statistically reliable (d+ = 0.31 for old vs. d+ = 0.14 for young; Z = 2.46, p < .007). A final meta-analysis of partial correlations from hierarchical logistic regressions — which simultaneously control for age and education — shows that the unique effect of age on response order susceptibility remains significant (r = .028, Z = 2.50, p < .006), while the unique effect of education does not reach significance in any analysis (all Z's < 1). The unique age effect is significantly larger than the unique education effect (Z = 1.63, p < .05).

The divorce experiment (September 1979) provides a striking illustration: for respondents under 55, the response order effect is small and non-significant (7.4%). For respondents 65+, the effect is 45.5% (χ² = 10.98, p < .007). Crucially, because older respondents disproportionately endorse whichever option was read last, the apparent age gap in attitudes toward divorce (68% vs. 36% favoring "more difficult" in one condition) reverses entirely when option order is changed — making the attitudinal conclusion entirely an artifact of order.

Knäuper proposes that age-related decline in working memory capacity is the mechanism: older respondents have less capacity to simultaneously store, retrieve, and elaborate all presented alternatives, and so fall back on the first or last item heard.
