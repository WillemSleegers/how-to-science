Smyth, Israel, Newberry, and Hull (2019) examined both response option order effects and stem order effects (the order in which the question stem/item content is presented) on five-point satisfaction scales. Data came from four surveys conducted in 2015–2016: two waves of the Family and Consumer Education Survey (FCES), one Family and Medically Needy Program (FMNP) survey, and a National Agricultural Statistics survey (NASIS). These used a mixed-mode design combining web and mail administration. Eight satisfaction items were analyzed, each measuring different aspects of program satisfaction.

Consistent response option order effects were found across items: respondents gave higher satisfaction ratings when the positive end of the scale appeared first (primacy effect). Stem order effects were found for four of the eight items — the meaning of the item influenced how sensitive responses were to the order in which the stem content was presented. The two factors (response option order and stem order) did not interact significantly. The study demonstrates that both scale design choices independently affect satisfaction survey results.

**Study details**
- Topic/construct: Program satisfaction (8 items across different federal/state programs)
- Response options: 5-point satisfaction scale (horizontal and vertical)
- Scale format: Mixed horizontal and vertical; both web and paper mail
- Design: Split-ballot between-subjects (response option order × stem order)
- Sample: Multiple surveys 2015–2016 (total N across surveys: thousands); mixed web + mail
- Mode: Web and mail
- Main finding: Consistent response option order effects (positive-first → higher ratings); stem order effects for 4/8 items; no interaction
