Krosnick (1991) introduced the satisficing framework to explain systematic biases in survey responding. The paper distinguishes between "optimizing" (the theoretically ideal response process) and "satisficing" — both weak (selecting the first acceptable response) and strong (providing minimally acceptable responses such as "don't know"). Krosnick argues that answering survey questions carefully is cognitively demanding and that respondents vary in their ability and motivation to complete the full optimal response process.

The satisficing account generates specific predictions about response-order effects. For visual modes (self-administered paper or web surveys), respondents process response options sequentially from the beginning, and satisficing respondents select the first plausible option they encounter — producing primacy effects. For auditory modes (telephone or interviewer-administered surveys), recency effects are expected because the last options heard remain most accessible in working memory. The paper outlines moderators: respondents lower in cognitive ability (e.g., lower education, older age), lower in motivation, or facing more cognitively demanding questions are more likely to satisfice. Krosnick reviews supporting evidence from existing studies but presents no new primary empirical data.

**Claim** — Satisficing respondents select the first acceptable response option rather than optimizing their answer, producing primacy effects in visual modes and recency effects in auditory modes.
**Quote** — "When response alternatives are presented to respondents visually, either on a showcard or in a self-administered questionnaire, weak satisficing is likely to bias respondents toward selecting response alternatives presented early in a list."

**Claim** — Questionnaire design features (question length, complexity, ambiguity) and respondent characteristics (cognitive ability, motivation) moderate the tendency to satisfice.
**Quote** — "The likelihood of satisficing is a joint function of task difficulty and the respondent's ability and motivation to optimize."
