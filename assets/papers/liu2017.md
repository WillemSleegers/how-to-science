Liu and Keusch (2017) examined the effect of rating scale direction on response styles — specifically acquiescent response style (ARS) and extreme response style (ERS) — comparing web and face-to-face interview modes. Data came from the 2012 American National Election Studies (ANES), which included a scale direction experiment. Respondents were randomly assigned to receive five-point fully verbalized agree/disagree scales in either descending (agree-first) or ascending (disagree-first) order.

In the web survey, the descending (agree-first) scale direction significantly increased ARS — respondents were more likely to select agreement options — compared to the ascending condition. This pattern is consistent with a combination of satisficing (selecting the first plausible option) and acquiescence bias (a pre-disposition to agree). ERS did not show a significant effect of scale direction. In the face-to-face (CAPI) mode, there was no significant effect of scale direction on either ARS or ERS. The authors attribute the null finding in face-to-face mode to the simultaneous use of both visual (show cards) and auditory (interviewer reading) modalities, which may attenuate primacy effects.

**Study details**
- Topic/construct: Attitudes toward traditionalism, position of Blacks, egalitarianism
- Response options: 5-point fully verbalized agree/disagree (descending vs ascending)
- Scale format: Vertical (web) and show card (face-to-face)
- Design: Split-ballot between-subjects (scale direction) × mode (web vs face-to-face)
- Sample: 2012 ANES; web N ≈ 3,500, face-to-face N ≈ 2,300 US adults
- Mode: Web and face-to-face (CAPI with CASI)
- Main finding: Scale direction increases ARS in web but not face-to-face; no effect on ERS
