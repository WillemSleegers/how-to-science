Krebs and Bachner (2018) tested whether response order effects in rating scales differ between German students (who read left to right) and Israeli students (who read right to left in Hebrew). Both groups completed paper questionnaires containing 7-point endpoint-labeled unipolar scales measuring motivation-related items in a split-ballot design: decremental (positive-first) vs. incremental (negative-first) scale direction.

Contrary to predictions that reading direction would moderate response order effects (since right-to-left readers might process the right side of a scale first), both German and Israeli respondents showed similar response order effects. The positive-first (decremental) scale yielded higher ratings in both samples. This finding suggests that response order effects on rating scales are not simply a product of reading habits transferring to scale processing. Instead, the effects appear to be driven by a more universal anchoring-and-adjustment or primacy mechanism that operates similarly regardless of reading direction.

**Claim** — Response order effects are found similarly in both left-to-right (German) and right-to-left (Israeli) reading direction samples, suggesting the effect is not driven by habitual reading direction.
