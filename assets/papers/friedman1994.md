Friedman, Herskovitz, and Pollack (1993) tested whether reversing the direction of a horizontal 5-point agree/disagree Likert scale would affect responses. Two hundred and eight undergraduate students at a large urban US college were randomly assigned to one of two questionnaires: one with response categories ordered "strongly agree … strongly disagree" (SA/SD) from left to right, and one with the reverse ordering (SD/SA). Both questionnaires contained ten items rating students' attitudes toward their college, including both favorably and unfavorably worded items. The scoring (1 = strongly agree, 5 = strongly disagree) was held constant across conditions.

A MANOVA across all ten items was significant (Wilks' Lambda = 0.91, F(10,197) = 2.04, p < .05), indicating that the two scale conditions produced significantly different response vectors. At the univariate level, three of the ten items showed significant differences: "courses are useful," "college has excellent reputation," and "college offers a broad selection of courses." In each case, the SA/SD scale yielded lower mean scores (i.e., more agreement) than the SD/SA scale, demonstrating a bias toward the left side of the horizontal scale. Notably, the effect appeared only for favorably worded items — none of the unfavorably worded items reached significance. The authors suggest that because students generally held positive attitudes, they readily endorsed favorable statements through left-side selection, whereas unfavorable items required more active cognitive processing that overrode the positional bias.

**Claim** — Reversing the order of response categories on a horizontal Likert scale produces significantly different results; responses are biased toward whichever category is placed on the left.
**Quote** — "This indicates the existence of a bias towards the response category that is listed first (i.e. on the left side) on the horizontally presented agreement scale."

**Claim** — The left-side bias manifested only for favorably worded items, not for unfavorably worded items, in a sample that generally held positive attitudes.
**Quote** — "This bias towards the left side of the scale appeared to manifest itself when the items were worded favorably."

**Study details**
- Topic/construct: Student attitudes toward college
- Response options: 5-point fully verbalized agree/disagree (SA/SD or SD/SA)
- Scale format: Horizontal
- Design: Split-ballot between-subjects
- Sample: N = 208 undergraduate students, large urban US college
- Mode: Paper questionnaire
- Main finding: SA/SD condition yielded significantly more agreement on favorably worded items; left-side primacy effect confirmed
