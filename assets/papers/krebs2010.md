Krebs and Hoffmeyer-Zlotnik (2010) tested whether the direction of an 8-point endpoint-labeled rating scale (labeled only at the endpoints: "not at all important" to "very important") affected responses to a battery of job characteristic importance items. Four hundred and thirty-six German university students completed paper questionnaires in a split-ballot design: half received a scale running positive-first (high importance to low importance) and half received the scale running negative-first (low importance to high importance).

The positive-first (decremental) condition yielded more positive responses overall — respondents endorsed higher importance ratings when the positive pole appeared at the left/start of the scale. This pattern is consistent with a primacy/anchoring effect. However, the authors also examined the factor structure of the resulting data and found no major differences between scale conditions in the underlying factor structure, suggesting that while mean levels differ, the dimensional structure of the construct was not substantially altered by scale direction. The study supports the conclusion that scale direction produces bias in means but does not fundamentally distort the measurement model.
