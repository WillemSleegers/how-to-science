Yan, Keusch, and He (2018) used secondary data analysis of the 2008 and 2012 American National Election Studies (ANES) to systematically examine which question-level and scale-level characteristics moderate scale direction effects. Both time series studies included split-ballot experiments varying scale direction (forward/high-to-low vs. reversed/low-to-high) for hundreds of survey items. The 2008 CAPI study included 2,322 respondents and 253 items; the 2012 study included 5,914 respondents (face-to-face CAPI and web) and 375 items.

Overall, 25.2% of the 628 items exhibited significant scale direction effects (primacy), with only 12 showing recency effects. Characteristics associated with stronger scale direction effects were: (1) non-attitudinal question type (behavioral/subjective mixed items: 37.5% showed effects vs. 23.8% attitudinal); (2) question location in the first half of the questionnaire (30.2% vs. 20.0% for second half); and (3) longer scales — 5+ point scales showed significantly more effects than 3- or 4-point scales. Scale type (attitudinal, frequency, quantity, evaluative) did not moderate scale direction effects. Self-administered items showed stronger scale direction effects than CAPI items (34.9% vs. 20.3%). The moderating effects of question type, location, and scale length were more pronounced in CAPI than in self-administered modes.

**Claim** — Scale direction effects are stronger for non-attitudinal items, items appearing earlier in the questionnaire, and items on longer scales.
**Quote** — "Scale direction effects are stronger for non-attitudinal items, earlier survey items, and items with longer scales."

**Study details**
- Topic/construct: Multiple political and social attitude items (ANES)
- Response options: Various (3-, 4-, 5-, 6-point scales); attitudinal, frequency, quantity, evaluative
- Scale format: CAPI show cards and web (self-administered)
- Design: Split-ballot within secondary ANES data; 628 items coded and analyzed
- Sample: 2008 ANES N = 2,322 (CAPI); 2012 ANES N = 5,914 (CAPI + web)
- Mode: CAPI (face-to-face) and web (self-administered)
- Main finding: Scale direction effects more likely for non-attitudinal items, earlier items, and longer scales; self-administered stronger than CAPI; anchoring favored over satisficing
