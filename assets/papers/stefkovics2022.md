Stefkovics (2022) examined scale direction effects across three survey modes (computer-assisted personal interviewing/CAPI, computer-assisted telephone interviewing/CATI, and an online non-probability panel) and three scale types (dichotomous yes/no, 1–4 Likert scale, and 0–10 numeric rating scale) in Hungary. The CAPI sample comprised approximately 2,000 adults, the CATI sample approximately 1,000 adults, and the online panel several hundred respondents. Split-ballot manipulation varied the direction (positive-first vs. negative-first or high-to-low vs. low-to-high) of response options or scale anchors.

Scale direction effects were rare in the interviewer-administered modes (CAPI and CATI): neither the dichotomous nor the Likert items showed consistent effects in telephone or face-to-face conditions. However, in the online panel survey, the 0–10 numeric rating scale showed significant scale direction effects — respondents gave higher ratings when the scale started with 10 than when it started with 0. Stefkovics argues that anchoring-and-adjustment provides a better theoretical foundation for explaining effects on numeric scales than satisficing, because the 0–10 scale is scalar and continuous rather than a list of categorical options to be sequentially processed.

**Study details**
- Topic/construct: Various attitudes and evaluations
- Response options: Dichotomous (yes/no), 1–4 Likert, 0–10 numeric rating scale
- Scale format: CAPI (laptop/tablet), CATI (telephone), online non-probability panel
- Design: Split-ballot between-subjects (scale direction) across three modes
- Sample: CAPI N ≈ 2,000; CATI N ≈ 1,000; online non-probability panel — Hungarian adults
- Mode: CAPI, CATI, online
- Main finding: Few scale direction effects in interviewer modes; 0–10 scale in online shows effects; anchoring-and-adjustment favored over satisficing for numeric scales
