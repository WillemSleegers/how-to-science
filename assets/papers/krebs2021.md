Krebs and Höhne (2019/2021) tested whether device type (PC vs. smartphone) and scale direction (decremental vs. incremental) interacted in a web survey. The study used a German non-probability access panel (N = 3,401) and presented 7-point endpoint-labeled vertical rating scales for motivation-related items. A 2×2 design varied device type (PC vs. smartphone) and scale direction (decremental/positive-first vs. incremental/negative-first).

Contrary to predictions based on device-specific differences in visual processing, both PC and smartphone respondents were similarly robust against scale direction effects — neither showed substantial differences between decremental and incremental scale conditions. This suggests vertical rating scales may be less prone to scale direction effects than horizontal scales. However, a significant main effect of device was found: PC respondents gave lower mean ratings than smartphone respondents overall, independent of scale direction. This device-level difference in response tendencies is an important finding for mixed-device web surveys.

**Claim** — Both PC and smartphone respondents are largely unaffected by scale direction on vertical endpoint-labeled scales, but PC respondents give lower ratings overall than smartphone respondents.
**Quote** — "Neither PC nor smartphone respondents showed substantial scale direction effects, but PC respondents gave lower mean ratings than smartphone respondents."

**Study details**
- Topic/construct: Motivation-related items
- Response options: 7-point endpoint-labeled vertical scale
- Scale format: Vertical, web
- Design: 2×2 between-subjects (device: PC vs smartphone × scale direction: decremental vs incremental)
- Sample: N = 3,401 German non-probability online access panel
- Mode: Web
- Main finding: Minimal scale direction effects for both devices; PC respondents give lower ratings than smartphone respondents regardless of scale direction
