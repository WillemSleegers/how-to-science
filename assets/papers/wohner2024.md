Wöhner and Franzen (2024) conducted an online survey experiment with 3,138 Swiss respondents testing the effects of scale direction (ascending 0–10 vs. descending 10–0) and scale orientation (horizontal vs. vertical) on subjective life satisfaction, measured using an 11-point endpoint-labeled numeric scale.

The descending scale (10 at the left/top) produced mean life satisfaction scores approximately 0.7 points lower than the ascending scale (0 at the left/top), a statistically significant and practically meaningful difference (p < 0.001). Additionally, the ascending scale showed higher construct validity — responses correlated more strongly with theoretically related measures. There was no significant main effect of scale orientation (horizontal vs. vertical), and the scale direction × orientation interaction was also non-significant. The authors recommend using ascending scales (lower values first) as they produce more valid and less biased life satisfaction data.

**Study details**
- Topic/construct: Subjective life satisfaction
- Response options: 11-point numeric endpoint-labeled scale (0–10 ascending vs 10–0 descending)
- Scale format: Horizontal and vertical (2×2 design with direction)
- Design: 2×2 between-subjects (scale direction × orientation)
- Sample: N = 3,138 Swiss adults (online survey)
- Mode: Web
- Main finding: Descending scale produces lower satisfaction scores; ascending scale more valid; orientation has no effect
