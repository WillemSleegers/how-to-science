Saris, Revilla, Krosnick, and Shaeffer (2010) compared the measurement quality of agree/disagree (A/D) and item-specific (IS) survey questions using a split-ballot multitrait-multimethod (MTMM) design embedded in the European Social Survey (ESS). The experiment spanned multiple European countries. Respondents were randomly assigned to either A/D versions (statements with "strongly agree … strongly disagree" response categories) or IS versions (questions with content-matched response categories) of the same underlying constructs.

Measurement quality was evaluated using structural equation modeling to estimate reliability and validity coefficients from the MTMM data. The results showed that A/D questions had substantially lower quality (lower reliability and lower validity) than IS questions. A major source of error in A/D questions was acquiescence — the tendency to agree regardless of content — which inflated spurious variance and reduced validity. IS questions were more reliably answered and produced more valid variance. The authors conclude that A/D questions are fundamentally inferior and advocate for the widespread adoption of IS question formats.
