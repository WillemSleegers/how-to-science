# Peytchev et al. (2010)

**Full citation:** Peytchev, A., Conrad, F. G., Couper, M. P., & Tourangeau, R. (2010). Increasing respondents' use of definitions in web surveys. *Journal of Official Statistics, 26*(4), 633–650.

## Study overview

- **N:** 2,481 completions (2,719 started)
- **Sample:** Opt-in web panel — two sources: Survey Sampling International (SSI) Survey Spot panel and AOL Opinion Place pop-up invitations. Not a probability sample; randomization rather than representation was the goal.
- **Mode:** Web survey
- **Survey topic:** Health and lifestyles (conducted December 2004 – January 2005)
- **Clarification type:** Definitions for 8 food/nutrition questions (same questions as Conrad et al. 2006, Study 2): dietary supplements, poultry, fat, cholesterol, and four others
- **Definition content:** Designed to shift responses relative to no-definition control by including counter-intuitive information (e.g., good vs. bad cholesterol; vegetables can have adverse effects when fried)

## Design

3 (definition condition) × 2 (definition length) × 2 (training) factorial design + control:

**Definition conditions:**
1. **Control** (20% of respondents): no definitions
2. **Rollover** (40%): definitions appeared when respondent hovered mouse over underlined term
3. **Always-on** (40%): definitions displayed below each question at all times

**Definition length:**
- Short: ~37 words on average
- Long: ~126 words on average (extended the short version without introducing new information)

**Training:**
- Half of rollover/always-on respondents completed training items before the 8 experimental questions — an assertion + definition of "artificial sweeteners" + 2 questions — designed to raise awareness that definitions help
- Half received no training

**Measurement:** Behavioral paradata (recorded rollovers via client-side JavaScript) + self-report at end of survey ("Did you ever consult definitions?") + response time per page

## Key findings

### Definition use
| Condition | Measure | Rate |
|---|---|---|
| Always-on | Self-report | 60.7% |
| Rollover | Behavioral (recorded rollovers) | 45.4% |
| Rollover | Self-report | 35.6% |

- Always-on respondents consulted definitions nearly twice as often as rollover respondents
- Among rollover respondents, those who reported using definitions spent ~26 seconds more on those pages than those who did not (confirming self-reports are meaningful)

### Training effect (counterintuitive)
- Respondents who received training used definitions **less**: 38% rollover rate among trained vs. 53% among untrained
- Interpretation: training made the effort of definition use more salient, leading respondents to opt out

### Definition length
- No effect of definition length on usage rate (short vs. long definitions requested equally often)
- No effect of length on response distributions

### Response distributions
- Definitions changed response patterns on **4 of 8 questions** in the always-on condition (vs. control)
- Definitions changed response patterns on **2 of 8 questions** in the rollover condition (vs. control)
- Questions affected: dietary supplements, poultry, fat, cholesterol

### Response times
- Always-on condition: ~60 seconds more per page than control
- Rollover condition: ~7 seconds more per page than control
- The time difference was consistent across all reading-speed deciles, suggesting genuine engagement with definitions (not just a few slow readers)

### No effect on breakoffs or item nonresponse
- No significant differences in dropout or missing data between conditions

## Relevance to tooltip usage

Key reference for always-on vs. rollover comparison. Shows always-on display substantially increases definition use and has measurable impact on responses. Also provides the counterintuitive training finding (explicit instruction to use definitions reduces usage).
