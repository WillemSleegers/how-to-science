Chyung, Kennedy, and Campbell (2018) authored a practitioner-oriented review article synthesizing research on whether Likert-type response options should be presented in ascending (strongly disagree … strongly agree) or descending (strongly agree … strongly disagree) order. The article is part of a series from Boise State University's Organizational Performance and Workplace Learning department aimed at providing evidence-based guidance for survey design practitioners and researchers.

The review covers studies from Belson (1966) through Liu and Keusch (2017), summarizing findings on primacy effects, left-side selection bias, satisficing, and acquiescence bias. The authors note that a sufficient body of research shows descending-ordered scales generate more positive responses than ascending-ordered scales — primarily through a primacy/left-side bias in self-administered visual surveys. They draw on Krosnick's satisficing theory and social desirability bias to explain why respondents select the first positive option when it appears on the left. They also review dissenting findings (including Weng and Cheng 2000, Christian et al. 2009, and Dillman et al. 2005) that failed to find significant order effects, noting these may reflect highly motivated samples or particular question types.

The article concludes with a practical recommendation: use ascending-ordered response scales (strongly disagree to strongly agree) for self-administered paper and web surveys to avoid inflated positive responses. If descending-ordered scales must be used, strategies include: splitting the sample between ascending and descending forms, or presenting options vertically rather than horizontally.
