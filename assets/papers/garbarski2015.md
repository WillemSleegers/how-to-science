Garbarski, Schaeffer, and Dykema (2015) conducted a 2×2 factorial experiment embedded in a large online panel survey (KnowledgePanel, N = 2,696 US adults). The two factors were: (1) response option order (excellent-to-poor vs. poor-to-excellent) for the standard five-category SRH question, and (2) question order (SRH asked first vs. SRH asked after a battery of domain-specific health questions about physical symptoms, limitations, and conditions).

Results showed significant main effects of both factors. Respondents reported higher (better) SRH when response options were ordered from excellent to poor, consistent with a primacy effect. They also reported higher SRH when SRH was asked first rather than after domain-specific health items. When SRH followed domain-specific health items, an assimilation effect occurred: respondents appeared to use the preceding health information to inform their SRH rating, pulling ratings toward their specific health status. The two factors did not interact significantly. The paper underscores that both scale design (response option order) and questionnaire context (question placement) independently shape SRH reports.

**Study details**
- Topic/construct: Self-rated health (SRH)
- Response options: 5-point (excellent / very good / good / fair / poor), order manipulated
- Scale format: Web radio buttons
- Design: 2×2 factorial between-subjects (response order × question order)
- Sample: N = 2,696 KnowledgePanel US adults
- Mode: Web
- Main finding: Primacy effect for response option order (excellent-first → better SRH); assimilation effect when SRH placed after domain-specific health items; no interaction
