Weng and Cheng (2000) tested whether changing the order of response options on a Likert-type scale would affect responses to a personal distress measure. Eight hundred and fifty-eight participants from Taiwan (490 college students and 368 junior high school students) completed a 5-point fully labeled Personal Distress Scale administered in paper questionnaire format. The study used a within-subjects design: participants completed the scale twice, with response option order (ascending vs. descending) varied across the two administrations.

The study found no substantial response order effects on means or on psychometric properties (reliability, factor structure). Neither the college nor the junior high school group showed significant differences based on scale direction. The authors attribute the absence of effects to the high motivation of their student samples — students completing a clearly labeled research survey were likely to engage fully with each item rather than satisficing. They emphasize that clear, unambiguous question wording and motivated respondents reduce the likelihood of response order effects.

**Claim** — Changing response option order (ascending vs. descending) had no substantial effect on means or psychometric properties in highly motivated Taiwanese student samples.
**Quote** — "Response order had no substantial effects on participant responses; psychometric properties were similarly unaffected."

**Study details**
- Topic/construct: Personal distress (psychological wellbeing scale)
- Response options: 5-point fully labeled scale (ascending vs descending order)
- Scale format: Paper questionnaire
- Design: Within-subjects (two administrations with different scale orders)
- Sample: N = 858 (490 college students, 368 junior high school students, Taiwan)
- Mode: Paper questionnaire (self-administered)
- Main finding: No substantial response order effects on means or psychometric properties; motivation of sample proposed as explanation
