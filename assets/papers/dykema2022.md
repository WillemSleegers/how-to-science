Dykema, Schaeffer, Garbarski, Assad, and Blixt (2022) present a comprehensive review and research synthesis examining the measurement properties and potential limitations of agree-disagree (AD) questions compared to item-specific (IS) questions. The paper synthesizes 20 experimental studies that directly compare AD and IS formats on data quality outcomes, presents conceptual models of the cognitive processing required for each format, and catalogs key question characteristics that typically differ between the two formats.

The review distinguishes between the two formats: AD questions ask respondents to agree or disagree with a statement (e.g., "Medical researchers work extremely hard… strongly agree … strongly disagree"), while IS questions directly ask about the underlying response dimension with tailored response categories (e.g., "How hard do medical researchers work… not at all hard … extremely hard"). The authors provide detailed cognitive processing models showing that AD questions require additional steps — threshold evaluation and polarity evaluation — that IS questions do not, making AD questions more cognitively burdensome.

On data quality outcomes, the weight of evidence favors IS questions. Three studies found higher validity for IS questions; no studies found higher validity for AD questions; five studies found higher reliability for IS questions. On undesirable indicators, four studies found more acquiescence for AD questions; three found primacy effects, extreme responding, or scale direction effects specifically for AD questions. Response times for initial AD items in batteries are shorter than for IS items, possibly because the repeated agree/disagree pattern becomes rote. However, this efficiency may be achieved at the cost of more superficial processing.

The authors catalog key question characteristics that commonly differ between AD and IS formats: manner of questioning (statement vs. question), response dimensions (AD offers agreement intensity, which often mismatches the underlying construct), threshold words, polarity (AD almost always bipolar; IS often unipolar), number and labeling of categories, direction of response categories, midpoint structure, battery grouping, and valence/alignment. They recommend IS questions for most research purposes and discuss challenges in translating existing AD scales to IS format.

**Claim** — AD questions require more complex cognitive processing than IS questions because they involve additional threshold and polarity evaluation steps not required by IS questions.
**Quote** — "AD questions present respondents with a mismatch between the question's 'offered' and 'underlying' response dimensions… These mismatches force respondents to undertake complicated cognitive processing steps."
