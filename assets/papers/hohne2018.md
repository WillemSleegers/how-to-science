Höhne and Krebs (published 2017) examined whether response order effects (decremental vs. incremental scale direction) differ between agree/disagree (A/D) and item-specific (IS) question formats. Nine hundred and thirty students at the University of Göttingen (Germany) completed paper questionnaires containing 5-point fully verbalized rating scales with no numeric values. Items tapping the same constructs were presented either as A/D questions (statements with agree/disagree response categories) or as IS questions (questions with response categories tailored to the content, e.g., frequency or quantity labels). Each format was presented in either decremental (positive-to-negative) or incremental (negative-to-positive) direction in a split-ballot design.

Response order effects (i.e., differences between decremental and incremental scale direction) were found for A/D questions but not for IS questions. For A/D questions, the decremental (agree-first) format produced systematically different responses than the incremental format. For IS questions, scale direction had no significant effect. The authors argue that the stronger susceptibility of A/D questions to scale direction effects is related to the greater ambiguity of agree/disagree response categories, which makes respondents more susceptible to primacy and anchoring processes, whereas IS categories provide clearer content anchors that reduce positional bias.

- Response options: 5-point fully verbalized, no numbers; A/D (agree/disagree) vs IS (item-specific content labels)
