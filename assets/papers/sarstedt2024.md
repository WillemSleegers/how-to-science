Sarstedt et al. (2024) review 28 articles (285 silicon-to-human comparisons) and propose guidelines for when silicon sampling is and is not appropriate in consumer and marketing research. Their main argument is that silicon samples are most valuable for upstream, exploratory tasks — pretesting survey instruments, generating scale items, stress-testing stimuli — rather than as substitutes for human data in confirmatory studies. They document consistent failures on implicit associations, interpersonal differences, non-WEIRD populations, and very recent phenomena, and provide practical decision rules for researchers considering the approach.

**Claim** — Silicon samples are most appropriate for upstream research tasks such as qualitative pretesting and pilot studies, where the goal is to collect information to safeguard follow-up design choices.
**Quote** — "We argue that silicon samples hold particular promise in upstream parts of the research process such as qualitative pretesting and pilot studies, where researchers collect external information to safeguard follow-up design choices."
**Page** — 1254

**Claim** — GPT can be used for scale pretesting: evaluating item wording, identifying double-barreled items, and flagging vague language.
**Quote** — "we recommend researchers to use silicon sampling for pretests and pilot studies, such as in scale pretesting where they could interrogate GPT whether a certain item wording is appropriate or not."
**Page** — 1256

**Claim** — Research topics well-represented in training data (e.g., customer satisfaction, service evaluations) are more likely to yield valid results; niche target audiences or very recent phenomena are not.
**Quote** — "research on customer satisfaction or service failures may have a relevant representation in the training data… while research on a very specific target audience (e.g., users of only a specific brand) may not be appropriately captured."
**Page** — 1260

**Claim** — Silicon samples cannot reliably replicate effects that rely on implicit associations, such as the endowment effect, mental accounting, or the sunk cost fallacy.
**Quote** — "researchers were unable to replicate effects known to characterize consumer behavior in many cases, such as the endowment effect, mental accounting, or the sunk cost fallacy (Chen et al., 2023)."
**Page** — 1255

**Claim** — GPT does not replicate risk preferences consistent with prospect theory; it maximises expected payoffs rather than acting risk-averse for gains and risk-seeking for losses.
**Quote** — "replicating Kahneman and Tversky's (1979) classic prospect theory experiment… Chen et al. (2023) found that ChatGPT mostly focuses on maximizing the expected payoffs, rather than, as humans do, acting risk-averse for gains and risk-seeking for losses."
**Page** — 1255

**Claim** — GPT performs poorly at mimicking interpersonal differences, which is crucial for psychological and marketing research.
**Quote** — "GPT, specifically, can hardly mimic interpersonal differences, which is crucial for psychological or marketing research studies"
**Page** — 1264

**Claim** — GPT's performance at mimicking human responses declines considerably for non-WEIRD populations.
**Quote** — "GPT's performance to mimic human responses declines considerably for non-WEIRD (Western, Educated, Industrialized, Rich, and Democratic) versus WEIRD countries."
**Page** — 1264

**Claim** — Researchers should always benchmark silicon samples against a human sample to avoid tapping into areas where LLMs and humans react differently.
**Quote** — "we recommend that researchers always benchmark their silicon samples with human samples to avoid the risk of tapping into an area where LLMs and humans react differently."
**Page** — 1263

**Claim** — Silicon samples that show no variation (a single "correct answer") should be disregarded, as this indicates the LLM is not capturing human-like response distributions.
**Quote** — (Table 2, Benchmarking): "Disregard silicon samples that do not show variation (but provide a single 'correct answer' (Park et al., 2023))."
**Page** — 1264

**Claim** — Researchers should never adjust prompts to generate a specific desired output content — this risks turning a confirmatory study into a p-hacking exercise.
**Quote** — "Under no circumstances, should researchers adjust prompts to generate a specific output content… Such practice could easily turn a confirmatory study into an exploratory fishing expedition—a practice that has been criticized as p-hacking"
**Page** — 1262

**Claim** — LLM training data does not represent a well-defined population; it is a massive collection of text from predominantly Western institutional sources.
**Quote** — "Training data sets, such as The Pile, are simply a collection of massive amounts of data without a clearly defined population… the data normally originates from Western institutions."
**Page** — 1260–1264

**Claim** — LLMs "are simply parroting what the training data tended to say about the concept so that the dialogue sounds natural" — the output does not necessarily reflect whose experiences or opinions it represents.
**Quote** — "LLMs 'are simply parroting what the training data tended to say about the concept so that the dialogue sounds natural' (Demszky et al., 2023, p. 4), but it is not clear whose experiences and opinions the output reflects."
**Page** — 1256

**Claim** — A systematic review of 28 articles (285 silicon-to-human comparisons) found that GPT replicated personality traits, framing effects, and political attitudes but failed on tasks requiring implicit associations; for 6 of 14 studies GPT gave virtually no response variation ("correct answer effect").
**Quote** — "The authors could only replicate just over a third of the results from the studies. Studies for which both the Many Labs 2 and the GPT samples replicated the original results rely on generalizing or comparing information that is provided directly in the task instruction. GPT, however, did not replicate effects that arise due to implicit associations."
**Page** — 1255
