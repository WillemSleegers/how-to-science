Yan and Keusch (2015) conducted an experiment embedded in a national US telephone survey (N = 498; RDD, CATI, April 2006) to test whether the direction of an 11-point end-labeled unipolar scale affected respondents' ratings of ten countries on a development scale (0 = least developed, 10 = most developed). Half of respondents received a 0-to-10 scale and half received a 10-to-0 scale. This was the first experiment manipulating the direction of an end-labeled unipolar scale in a telephone setting.

Results showed a significant scale direction effect for countries that received high development ratings. Specifically, three countries (Japan, China, Sweden) received significantly higher ratings in the 10-to-0 condition than the 0-to-10 condition, consistent with the hypothesis that ratings are biased toward the starting point. Countries with low ratings did not show significant effects.

To distinguish between satisficing and anchoring-and-adjustment as mechanisms, the authors tested individual-difference moderators. Respondents' knowledge of the topic (development) significantly moderated the effect: low-knowledge respondents showed scale-direction effects for all countries regardless of development status, while high-knowledge respondents showed the biasing effect only for developed countries. Age also showed a significant three-way interaction with scale direction and development status. Crucially, proxies for satisficing motivation (interest in the survey, interviewer ratings of engagement) did not significantly moderate scale direction effects. This pattern of moderators is more consistent with anchoring-and-adjustment (where knowledge of the anchor's precision matters) than satisficing (where cognitive capacity and motivation should matter).

**Claim** — Scale direction significantly affects ratings for countries with high development scores, biasing them toward the starting point of the scale.
**Quote** — "The direction of the rating scale significantly affects respondents' ratings of countries that received high ratings. Specifically, ratings for those countries are higher when the scale starts with a high number than when the scale begins with a low number."

**Claim** — Evidence favors anchoring-and-adjustment over satisficing as the mechanism: knowledge of the task (not survey motivation) moderates the scale direction effect.
**Quote** — "The only respondent characteristic that has a statistically significant two-way interaction with the scale direction is respondents' knowledge of development."

**Study details**
- Topic/construct: Country development ratings
- Response options: 11-point end-labeled unipolar scale (0 = least developed, 10 = most developed); direction manipulated (0-to-10 vs 10-to-0)
- Scale format: Verbal/telephone (end-labeled, read by interviewer)
- Design: Split-ballot between-subjects (0-to-10 vs 10-to-0)
- Sample: N = 498 US adults; RDD national telephone survey
- Mode: Telephone (CATI)
- Main finding: Primacy effect for high-rated countries; anchoring-and-adjustment supported over satisficing as mechanism
