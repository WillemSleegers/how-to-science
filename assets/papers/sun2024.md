description: Proposes "random silicon sampling" — using only group-level demographic distributions (not individual-level data) to simulate sub-population opinions; works well for overall population and some subgroups but fails on sensitive topics due to model harmlessness bias
type: reference

**Claim** — "Random silicon sampling" extends Argyle et al.'s silicon sampling by using only the demographic distribution of a target group (not individual-level data), making it feasible for contexts where individual respondent data is unavailable before a survey.
**Quote** — "Our study proposes 'random silicon sampling,' which generates the opinions correspond with a real human group through LLMs based on the demographic distribution of the group without requiring individual-level data to address this research gap."
**Page** — 1

**Claim** — On the overall ANES 2020 sample, random silicon sampling produced a Biden/Trump vote distribution with an average KL-divergence of 0.0004, which was more similar to actual ANES responses than Argyle et al.'s silicon sampling.
**Quote** — "Table 1 illustrates that, through random silicon sampling, the language model generated a response distribution that is remarkably similar to the actual ANES responses (average KL-divergence = 0.0004), exhibiting higher similarity than the silicon sample."
**Page** — 6

**Claim** — Results were reproducible across ten repeated random silicon samplings in the same setting: average Biden vote rate was 57.43% with a standard deviation of only 0.46%.
**Quote** — "the voting choice distributions from ten repeated random silicon samplings in the same setting demonstrated high consistency with an average vote rate for Biden of 57.43% and a standard deviation of 0.46%."
**Page** — 6

**Claim** — A minimum sample size of approximately 200 is required; replicability degraded below that threshold, with the 2% (n=108) sample incorrectly favouring Trump.
**Quote** — "replication was feasible with a sample size of 4% (217)... At 2% (=108), the random silicon sample incorrectly favored Donald Trump (51.58%) over Joe Biden (48.42%). A possible interpretation could be similar to the emphasis in social science research on possessing a minimum of 200 samples."
**Page** — 7

**Claim** — Party ID subgroups (Democrats, Republicans, Independents) were poorly replicated: Democratic and Republican simulated vote rates were 99.96% and 99.22% respectively — far more extreme than actual ANES distributions. Independents were the hardest group to replicate.
**Quote** — "The Joe Biden and Donald Trump voting rates among democratic and republican supporters in random silicon sample were 99.96% and 99.22%, respectively, which are more extreme than those in the actual ANES groups. Further, the model struggled the most in replicating the response distribution of the independent group."
**Page** — 6–7

**Claim** — Across 10 multiple-choice questions on different topics, random silicon sampling successfully replicated actual ANES distributions for only 1 out of 10 (income inequality). On sensitive topics, the model defaulted to "harmless" responses regardless of demographic conditioning.
**Quote** — "the random silicon sample can replicate opinions only for questions regarding 'income inequality' (p > 0.05, KL-divergence = 0.00096)... we speculate that this tendency reflects the GPT-3.5's bias towards 'harmlessness,' considering the sensitivity of the question topics."
**Page** — 7

**Claim** — The "harmlessness" bias is more extreme for certain demographic subgroups: Black and Democratic groups showed stronger over-correction toward innocuous responses than White and Republican groups (e.g., same-sex marriage approval: 95.67% for Black simulated respondents vs. 57.08% in actual ANES Black respondents).
**Quote** — "ANES responses from the white group showed a 70.43% approval rate for same-sex marriages, which was higher than the 57.08% observed for the black group. In a random silicon sample, the approval rate among the white group was 77.46%, whereas it was significantly higher in the black group at 95.67%."
**Page** — 8

**Claim** — Generating 5,441 synthetic responses cost only $0.70 using GPT-3.5-turbo, demonstrating cost efficiency as a practical advantage over traditional surveys.
**Quote** — "their cost-effectiveness (generating 5441 synthetic responses for only $0.7 in an hour) makes them invaluable for preliminary testing."
