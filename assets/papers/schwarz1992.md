Schwarz, Hippler, and Noelle-Neumann (1992) presented the cognitive elaboration model of response order effects in a theoretical chapter, supported by empirical data from the Institut für Demoskopie Allensbach using quota samples of approximately 2,000 West German adults. The chapter synthesizes research on how the mode of scale presentation (visual vs. auditory) interacts with respondents' cognitive processing to produce primacy or recency effects.

The cognitive elaboration model proposes that when response options are presented visually (as in self-administered paper or web surveys), respondents read through the options, elaborate on the first plausible item encountered, and are more likely to select it — producing primacy effects. When options are presented aurally (as in telephone surveys), the most recently heard item is most accessible in working memory, producing recency effects. When both visual and auditory presentation occur simultaneously (as in face-to-face interviews where both show cards and verbal reading occur), the recency effect from auditory presentation tends to dominate over the visual primacy effect. The empirical data from German surveys supported these predictions across several question types.

**Claim** — Visual presentation of response options produces primacy effects; auditory presentation produces recency effects; simultaneous visual+auditory presentation leads to recency dominance.
**Quote** — "When response alternatives are presented visually, primacy effects emerge; when presented auditorily, recency effects are obtained."

**Study details**
- Topic/construct: Multiple attitude items
- Response options: Various rating scale formats
- Scale format: Visual (self-administered) vs auditory (telephone/oral) vs combined
- Design: Multiple experiments with German adult quota samples
- Sample: ~2,000 West German adults per experiment (Institut für Demoskopie Allensbach)
- Mode: Various (mail/self-administered, telephone, face-to-face)
- Main finding: Visual → primacy; auditory → recency; combined → recency dominates
