Kim & Lee (2024) fine-tune LLMs on 50 years of General Social Survey data to predict individual survey responses, then aggregate using survey weights — contrasting with prompt-based silicon sampling that only produces group-level modal opinions. They achieve strong aggregate performance for questions already in the training data (ρ > 0.98), but performance drops sharply for truly unasked questions (ρ = 0.67, 12% of predictions within ±3%). The paper frames the approach as augmentation, not replacement, and explicitly warns against high-stakes use. A key limitation is that the model is less accurate for racial minorities, low-SES respondents, and politically uncorrelated opinions.

**Claim** — The paper fine-tunes LLMs on 50 years of GSS data to predict individual responses, then aggregates using survey weights — explicitly contrasting with prompt-based silicon sampling which produces only "typical" group-level opinions.
**Quote** — "LLMs can generate a typical opinion in a particular demographic group, though it is difficult to predict a specific individual's heterogenous opinion, consequently narrowing the distribution of responses within groups."
**Page** — 6–7

**Claim** — The paper introduces "retrodiction" — predicting individual responses for years when a question was not asked — and "unasked opinion prediction" — predicting responses to questions that were never asked of any respondent in the training data.
**Quote** — "We develop a novel methodological framework that fine-tunes LLMs with repeated cross-sectional surveys to incorporate the meaning of survey questions, individual beliefs, and temporal contexts for opinion prediction. We introduce two new emerging applications of the AI-augmented survey: retrodiction (i.e., predict year-level missing responses) and unasked opinion prediction (i.e., predict entirely missing responses)."
**Page** — 1

**Claim** — For missing data imputation, the best model (Alpaca-7b) achieves AUC = 0.866 at the individual level and ρ = 0.99 at the aggregate level; 51.7% of aggregate predictions fall within ±3% of the true value.
**Quote** — "Our top-performing model succeeds in the missing imputation task (AUC = 0.866), though the matrix factorization model also shows a similar level of performance (AUC = 0.852)."
**Page** — 13

**Claim** — For retrodiction, the model achieves AUC = 0.860 individually and ρ = 0.98 in aggregate, significantly outperforming matrix factorization (AUC = 0.798).
**Quote** — "Our model also succeeds in the retrodiction task (AUC = 0.860), where it needs to predict the entirely missing responses in a certain year, significantly outperforming the matrix factorization model (AUC = 0.798)."
**Page** — 13

**Claim** — For unasked opinion prediction, aggregate performance drops sharply to ρ = 0.67 and only 11.6% of predictions fall within ±3% — the authors caution against high-stakes use for this task.
**Quote** — "Our model shows a relatively lower correlation (ρ = 0.68) in unasked opinion prediction... our model with the 3% margin of error can predict only about 12% of true survey responses in the unasked opinion prediction task."
**Page** — 15

**Claim** — Opinions strongly correlated with political ideology are far more predictable (AUC up to 0.95) than politically uncorrelated opinions (AUC as low as 0.57 for work satisfaction).
**Quote** — "the association between individuals' dissatisfaction with work situations and their political ideology was extremely weak, with a correlation coefficient of less than 0.01. The model's accuracy in retrodicting this belief was also very low, yielding an AUC of 0.57."
**Page** — 21

**Claim** — The model predicts higher-SES and White respondents more accurately than lower-SES and minority respondents: those with a master's degree show 0.037 higher AUC than those without a high school diploma; Black respondents show 0.015 lower AUC than White respondents.
**Quote** — "opinions of individuals with higher socioeconomic status (SES)… are more predictable than those with lower SES… whereas those in the top 20% income bracket are more predictable than those in the bottom 20%, with a 0.023 higher AUC."
**Page** — 18

**Claim** — The paper's core framing is augmentation, not replacement: performance is strong when human survey data exists to train on, and modest when it does not.
**Quote** — "Our answer is that LLMs are more useful in augmenting, rather than completely replacing, human responses in surveys... personalized LLMs show modest capacity when human-generated responses are not readily available, such as unasked opinion prediction, challenging the idea of replacing human subjects with AI agents."
**Page** — 21–22

**Claim** — Lower accuracy for racial minorities and low-SES individuals means deploying these predictions in policy contexts risks further marginalising already underrepresented groups.
**Quote** — "Our model's lower accuracy for individuals with low socioeconomic status, racial minorities, and non-partisan affiliations can exacerbate the demographic representation issue. For instance, if decision-makers use these kinds of models to guide policy implementation, the less predictable voices of minority groups could be marginalized."
**Page** — 24
