Malhotra (2008) examined primacy effects in web surveys using data from Knowledge Networks (now GfK), a probability-based US adult online panel (N = 397). Respondents were randomly assigned to receive either positively ordered (decremental) or negatively ordered (incremental) versions of 5-point unipolar rating scales measuring attitudes about the government's response to Hurricane Katrina, as well as some 4-category items.

Primacy effects were found for the unipolar rating scales: respondents gave more positive ratings when the positive pole appeared first. The effect was moderated by a two-way interaction between completion speed and education: the primacy effect was strongest for respondents who completed the survey quickly (speeders) with low education. High-education respondents or slow completers showed smaller or negligible scale direction effects. This pattern supports the satisficing explanation: low-ability or low-motivation respondents are most susceptible to positional anchoring.

**Claim** — Primacy effects in web survey rating scales are moderated by completion speed and education; fast, low-education respondents show the largest effects.
**Quote** — "The primacy effect was strongest for respondents who completed the survey quickly with low education, supporting the satisficing account."

**Study details**
- Topic/construct: Attitudes toward government response to Hurricane Katrina
- Response options: 5-point unipolar rating scale (positive-first vs negative-first); also 4-category items
- Scale format: Web radio buttons
- Design: Split-ballot between-subjects (scale direction)
- Sample: N = 397 Knowledge Networks US adults
- Mode: Web
- Main finding: Primacy effects for unipolar rating scales; effect strongest among fast + low-education respondents; consistent with satisficing
