# Conrad et al. (2006)

**Full citation:** Conrad, F. G., Couper, M. P., Tourangeau, R., & Peytchev, A. (2006). Use and non-use of clarification features in web surveys. *Journal of Official Statistics, 22*(2), 245–269.

## Study overview

- **Mode:** Web survey (two separate experiments)
- **Survey topic:** Food and nutrition consumption ("lifestyle" survey)
- **Clarification type:** Hyperlinked definitions for food/nutrition terms (e.g., polyunsaturated fatty acid, vegetables, cholesterol)
- **Definition content:** Half were "useful" (counter-intuitive information likely to shift responses, e.g., vegetables include French fries) and half were "not useful" (confirmatory/neutral)

## Experiment 1

- **N:** ~2,871
- **Sample:** Online panel (web-based)
- **Interface conditions:** One-click, Two-click, Click-and-scroll
- **Concept conditions:** Technical terms (e.g., polyunsaturated fatty acid) vs. ordinary terms (e.g., vegetables)
- **Definition usefulness:** Useful (counter-intuitive, likely to shift responses) vs. not useful (confirmatory)
- **Key findings:**
  - Overall: only **13.8% of respondents** requested any definitions
  - Requests were far more common for **technical** than ordinary terms
  - **One-click** produced more requests than two-click or click-and-scroll
  - When respondents obtained a definition, response patterns differed from non-requesters — suggesting definitions were read and used

## Experiment 2

- **N:** ~3,050
- **Sample:** Online panel (web-based); plus a replication with ~941 Knowledge Networks probability-sample respondents
- **Interface conditions:** One-click, Two-click, Rollover (mouse hover)
- **Concept conditions:** Ordinary terms only (e.g., fat, vegetables, dairy, cholesterol) — deliberately more stringent than Experiment 1
- **Instruction conditions:** Encouraging ("please click on the word to obtain a definition") vs. neutral
- **Key findings:**
  - Overall: **22.4%** of respondents requested at least one definition
  - **Rollover:** 36.5% of respondents requested at least one definition
  - **One-click:** 8.9%
  - **Two-click:** 6.5%
  - **Instructions had no effect:** 18.1% (encouraging) vs. 16.8% (neutral) — not significant
  - Rollover respondents answered faster than click-based requesters despite having more definitions available
  - KN replication confirmed the pattern

## Relevance to tooltip usage

Core reference establishing that (1) voluntary use of tooltip-style clarification features is very low under click-based access, (2) usage is highly sensitive to interaction cost, and (3) rollover/hover dramatically outperforms click-based access.
