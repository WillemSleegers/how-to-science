Garbarski, Schaeffer, and Dykema (2016) report two replication studies examining the effect of response option order on self-rated health (SRH). Earlier research (including their own 2015 work) had shown that SRH is higher when response options are ordered from "excellent" to "poor" than when ordered from "poor" to "excellent." These studies aimed to replicate and extend those findings using probability-based online samples.

Study 1 used a TESS (Time-sharing Experiments for the Social Sciences) sample drawn from KnowledgePanel (N = 1,347 US adults). Study 2 used an RTI International panel sample (N = 499 US adults), also from KnowledgePanel. In both studies, respondents were randomly assigned to either an excellent-to-poor or poor-to-excellent ordering of the 5-point SRH response scale. Results replicated the earlier finding: mean SRH was significantly lower when the scale started with "poor" in both studies. The effect sizes were modest but consistent across both replication samples, supporting the robustness of the response order effect for the SRH question.

**Claim** — Respondents report lower self-rated health when the response scale begins with "poor" compared to when it begins with "excellent," replicating earlier findings in two independent probability-based online samples.
**Quote** — "Mean SRH was lower when the scale started with 'poor' in both studies."

**Study details**
- Topic/construct: Self-rated health (SRH)
- Response options: 5-point (excellent / very good / good / fair / poor), order manipulated
- Scale format: Web survey drop-down or radio buttons
- Design: Split-ballot between-subjects, two independent replication studies
- Sample: Study 1: N = 1,347 KnowledgePanel US adults (TESS); Study 2: N = 499 KnowledgePanel US adults (RTI)
- Mode: Web
- Main finding: Consistent primacy effect — excellent-to-poor order yields higher (better) SRH than poor-to-excellent order
