Rammstedt and Krebs (2007) examined how scale direction and the consistency between numeric and verbal labels affect responses to a personality scale. Three hundred and fifteen German university students completed an 8-point endpoint-labeled scale (the BFI-10 Big Five personality inventory) twice at two time points in a dependent (within-subjects) design. Scale conditions varied both the direction (positive-to-negative vs. negative-to-positive) and whether numeric values were consistent with the verbal labels or counterintuitive (e.g., high numbers aligned with the negative pole).

The key finding was that scale direction alone did not significantly affect personality responses — positive-first vs. negative-first ordering had no meaningful effect when numeric and verbal labels were consistent. However, a mismatch between the number direction and the verbal direction — presenting high numbers alongside the negative verbal pole (a counterintuitive arrangement) — did significantly affect responses. This suggests that respondents use both numeric and verbal cues to interpret scales, and inconsistencies between these two types of cues introduce measurement error.

**Claim** — Scale direction alone (positive-first vs negative-first) does not affect personality scale responses when number and label directions are consistent.
**Quote** — "Scale direction alone did not affect responses; mismatched number-verbal labeling significantly affected responses."
